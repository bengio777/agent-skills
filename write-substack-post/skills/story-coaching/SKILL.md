# Story Coaching

Coach the author through building a narrative structure using the Amuse-Bouche framework. Work through each component one at a time — propose, get the author's reaction, confirm, then move on.

## The Amuse-Bouche Framework

A short-form story has exactly four parts, built in this order:

1. **Anchor** (build first) — The single memorable moment the reader will carry with them. The surprising fact, revelation, or twist that justifies the whole piece.
2. **Essential Details** (build second) — Only the setup needed to make the Anchor land. "An amuse-bouche, not a 7-course meal."
3. **Dismount** (build third) — How it ends. A satisfying conclusion, a call-to-action, or a callback to the opening.
4. **Hook** (build last) — The opening that earns the reader's next 5 minutes. Written last because you need to know where the story goes before you can invite someone in.

## Workflow

### 1. Find the Anchor

Propose a candidate anchor moment based on the chosen story angle and media analysis.

Ask: "I think the moment that will stick with readers is [proposed anchor]. Does that feel right, or is the real moment something else?"

**If the author is stuck, probe with:**
- "What surprised you about this experience?"
- "What would you tell a friend first?"
- "What changed after this moment?"
- "What's the one thing you want someone to take away?"

Offer 2 candidate moments if needed. Confirm before moving on.

### 2. Build Essential Details

Draft 3-4 setup points that make the Anchor land.

**Enforce brevity ruthlessly:**
- If more than 4 setup points, challenge: "Which of these can the reader live without?"
- Test each detail: "Does the Anchor still land if we remove this?" If yes, cut it.
- Remind: "We're writing an amuse-bouche, not a 7-course meal. Every detail earns its place by serving the Anchor."

Present the trimmed essential details. Confirm before moving on.

### 3. Craft the Dismount

Propose an ending that matches the tone of the Anchor. Three options:

- **Satisfying conclusion** — The story resolves naturally. The reader feels complete.
- **Call-to-action** — Invite the reader to do something, reflect, or engage.
- **Callback** — Circle back to something from the opening or the Anchor. Creates a feeling of wholeness.

Recommend the best fit with reasoning. Ask: "Does this ending land for you, or should we try a different approach?"

### 4. Write the Hook (Last)

Now that the destination is clear, recommend a hook type:

| Hook Type | Best When | Example Pattern |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Dialogue** | The story starts with a conversation or a voice | "She looked at me and said..." |
| **Descriptive/Snapshot** | The setting or scene is vivid and essential | Drop the reader into a specific moment — sights, sounds, textures |
| **Bold Direct Statement** | You have a provocative claim or counterintuitive insight | Lead with the most surprising thing. No preamble. |
| **Emotional/Heartfelt** | The feeling IS the story | Open with how you felt — raw, honest, specific |
| **Question** | You want the reader to see themselves in the story | Ask something they can't ignore, that connects to their own experience |

Recommend the best fit. Draft 1-2 hook options for the author to react to.

Ask: "Which of these pulls you in? Or should we try a different approach?"

## Output

After all four components are confirmed, present the complete narrative structure:

**Your story structure:**
- **Hook:** [Confirmed hook]
- **Essential Details:** [Confirmed setup points]
- **Anchor:** [Confirmed memorable moment]
- **Dismount:** [Confirmed ending]

"This is the skeleton. Ready to draft?"

## Guidelines

- One component at a time — never present all four at once
- Always propose first, then ask for reaction
- Protect the author's instincts — if they feel strongly about something, go with it even if it's not textbook
- Keep the coaching conversational: "here's what I'm thinking" not "the framework dictates"
- If the author is stuck for more than 2 exchanges on any component, offer a concrete alternative rather than more questions
